PROLOGUE
A Note on the Sanskrit

The well-formed Sanskrit pronunciation is Oṃ Maṇi Padme Hūṃ. In the general sense, Oṃ corresponds to the Sanskrit praṇama — profound praise, homage, and prostration — and it also conveys auspiciousness and good fortune.

Held in this single utterance is the entire path: the recognition of the impure, the invocation of the pure, and the means by which one becomes the other. What follows is an unfolding of that meaning, syllable by syllable.

PART I

The Words Themselves

1. The Six Syllables and Their Direct Meaning

Before entering the symbolism, it is worth meeting the words plainly. Each syllable carries a literal sense in Sanskrit which the deeper teachings then unfold.

Oṃ (oṃ) · Composed of the three letters A, U, and MA — the seed of body, speech, and mind.

Maṇi (ma·ṇi) · Jewel.

Padma (pad·ma) · Lotus flower. The long e in Padme is a vocative form — like a child calling "Ma!" with a drawn-out voice — representing the eighth grammatical case, the case of calling.

Hūṃ (hūṃ) · A word of urging and summoning. According to many scriptural sources it is also the seed syllable of the mind, or heart.

Read together, the mantra is an invocation: "O Kye! Holder of the Jewel and the Lotus" — addressed to the deity who holds a crystal rosary and a golden lotus as hand attributes — and an urging of the enlightened heart: "Please bestow upon me the siddhis of body, speech, and mind!"

2. Oṃ — The Three Letters A, U, and MA

At the heart of Oṃ lie the three letters A, U, and MA. These three letters carry a double burden of meaning. They represent both the impure and the pure — both what we are now and what we may become.

The Two Sets of Three Doors

◆ The Impure Three Doors

The ordinary, deluded body, speech, and mind of the practitioner.

◆ The Pure Three Doors

The enlightened body, speech, and mind of a Buddha — also called the Three Vajras.

The key question is this: can the impure three doors be transformed into the very essence of the pure three doors, or are they fundamentally different from the start?

The Buddhist teaching does not claim that there exists, from the very beginning, an entity already fully endowed with all qualities and free from every fault. All Buddhas were once just like us — and by relying on the path they attained enlightenment. The pure body, speech, and mind come about by gradually relinquishing the impure natures and transforming them into pure natures.

The very path that achieves this transformation is indicated by the remaining four syllables of the mantra — Maṇi and Padma (method and wisdom), and Hūṃ (their union).

3. Maṇi — The Jewel of Bodhicitta

Just as a wish-fulfilling jewel fulfills the desires of beings, so the jewel of bodhicitta fulfills the aims of all who seek liberation.

Maṇi means jewel. It symbolises the method aspect of the path: the mind of enlightenment (bodhicitta), love, and compassion.

Just as a precious jewel has the power to dispel poverty, the jewel of bodhicitta has the power to dispel every kind of decline — the decline of saṃsāra and the limited peace of nirvāṇa alike. And just as a wish-fulfilling jewel fulfills the desires of sentient beings, bodhicitta fulfills their deepest aims.

This is why the syllable Maṇi is placed at the very gate of the path: without compassion as the ground, no further step has direction.

4. Padma — The Lotus of Wisdom

Padma means lotus flower, and it symbolises wisdom.

A lotus is born from mud yet remains completely unstained by it. In the same way, genuine wisdom has the power to remain in a state where contradictions do not arise — it does not fall into extremes. Where wisdom is lacking, contradictions inevitably arise.

The Many Wisdoms — and the Principal One

The tradition speaks of many kinds of wisdom:

  • the wisdom that realises impermanence;
  • the wisdom that realises the selflessness of persons;
  • the wisdom that realises twofold emptiness — that subject and object are not substantially different;
  • the wisdom that realises the emptiness of things established by their own characteristics.

Among all of these, the principal wisdom indicated by Padma is the wisdom that directly realises emptiness — śūnyatā itself, seen face to face.

5. Hūṃ — The Indivisible Union

The final syllable Hūṃ indicates the indivisible essence — the union of method and wisdom. It teaches that the purification of the faults in one's own mindstream must necessarily be accomplished through that union; neither method alone nor wisdom alone is sufficient.

Two Perspectives on the Union

From the Sūtra Perspective

This union is wisdom embraced by method — compassion infused with insight, insight infused with compassion.

From the Mantra (Vajrayāna) Perspective

It refers to a single consciousness that is the fully perfected, indivisible aspect of method and wisdom together — not two factors held side by side, but one inseparable awareness.

Additionally, according to the seed syllables of the five buddha families, Hūṃ is the seed syllable of the Unshakable Conqueror Akṣobhya — the one who cannot be perturbed by anything.

Therefore, the six syllables Oṃ Maṇi Padme Hūṃ teach that by relying on the practice of the path where method and wisdom are united, one's own impure body, speech, and mind can be utterly transformed into the very essence of a Buddha's pure body, speech, and mind.

PART II

The Complete Purification System

Each syllable of the mantra functions within a complete fourfold framework. It corresponds to a class of beings whose suffering it pacifies, an affliction it purifies, a perfection it brings to maturity, and a buddha-family whose enlightened quality it reveals.

The traditional colouring of the six syllables is therefore not decorative — each colour directly symbolises one of the six Conqueror families.

6. The Six-Syllable Framework

The table below brings the four dimensions of purification into a single view. Read across each row to see how one syllable simultaneously addresses a realm of beings, an inner affliction, a perfection of the path, and the family of awakening it reveals.

The Fourfold Function of Each Syllable

Syllable Realm Pacified Affliction Purified Perfection Completed Buddha Family
Oṃ Gods — death and transmigration Pride Generosity Vairocana (white)
Ma Demigods — strife and quarrelling Jealousy Moral discipline Amoghasiddhi (green)
Ṇi Humans — birth, aging, sickness, death Desire Patience Ratnasambhava (yellow)
Pad Animals — bondage and exploitation Ignorance Joyous effort Akṣobhya (blue)
Me Hungry ghosts — hunger and thirst Miserliness Meditative concentration Amitābha (red)
Hūṃ Hell beings — heat and cold Hatred / anger Wisdom Vajradhara (dark blue)

Notice how the structure moves from basis (the realm), through what is purified (the affliction), to the path (the perfection), and finally to the result (the buddha family). The mantra is, in this sense, the entire path compressed into six syllables.

PART III

Practice and Benefits

7. How to Meditate and Recite

The traditional method of recitation is rooted in visualization. The recitation is not merely vocal; it is a single act of body, speech, and mind held together.

The Visualization

Meditate upon your guru and the Noble Avalokiteśvara as inseparable. At their heart, upon a moon disc, visualize the white seed syllable Hrīḥ — the white Hrīḥ symbolizes the heart of Avalokiteśvara. This syllable is itself a seed of the heart, derived from the Sanskrit hṛdaya, meaning heart, essence, or core. Around the central Hrīḥ, visualize the six syllables circling.

Focus single-pointedly on this and perform the recitation.

8. The Benefits of Each Syllable

If the six-syllable vidyā-mantra is recited correctly, the benefits are immeasurable. To summarize, each syllable grants a specific quality, set out in the table below. The right-hand column traces the corresponding stage on the five paths of the bodhisattva — a sweep from beginner's ground to omniscience.

What Each Syllable Bestows

Syllable Perfection Siddhi Bestowed Affliction Cleansed Quality of Avalokiteśvara
Oṃ Generosity The supreme siddhi Ignorance Body · Path of Accumulation
Ma Moral discipline The common siddhis Anger Speech · Path of Preparation
Ṇi Patience Pacifying illness and harmful spirits Miserliness Mind · Path of Seeing
Pad Joyous effort Increasing life and merit Ignorance Qualities · Path of Meditation
Me Concentration Bringing people, wealth, and resources under control Jealousy Activity · Path of No-More-Learning
Hūṃ Wisdom Destroying enemies, obstructers, and malevolent forces Pride Deeds · Omniscience

Through this single mantra, then, the six root afflictions are purified, the six perfections are brought to completion, and the six families of enlightened qualities are realized — a complete path, recited in a breath.

◆ ◆ ◆

EPILOGUE
The Seed Within

The state of Buddhahood need not be sought somewhere outside. The cause, the very potential for attaining it, exists already within one's own mindstream.

As the protector Maitreya stated in the Mahāyānottaratantra — the Sublime Continuum — all beings intrinsically possess buddha-nature (tathāgatagarbha). That pure seed — the very essence of the Tathāgata, from which the fully perfected state is accomplished — is already present in each of us.

The six syllables Oṃ Maṇi Padme Hūṃ are the direct method for revealing it. Spoken with attention, they are not a request for something distant. They are a recognition of something already here.

DEDICATION

By whatever merit may arise from compiling and reading these words,

may all beings be freed from suffering and the causes of suffering,

and may they swiftly attain the state of the Noble Avalokiteśvara.

序
梵文發音說明

此咒之正確梵文發音為 Oṃ Maṇi Padme Hūṃ（唵嘛呢叭咪吽）。就一般意義而言,「唵」字對應梵文 praṇama，意為深切之讚歎、頂禮與恭敬,並蘊含吉祥圓滿之義。

此六字真言之中,涵攝整條道路 ── 對不淨之認識、對清淨之祈請,以及由前者轉成後者之方法。以下將依序展開每一字之深義。

第一部

字義本身

第一章 六字之直接含義

進入象徵意義之前,先須了解每字之直接梵文義,而後深義方可一一展開。

唵 (Oṃ) 由「阿」、「烏」、「嘛」三字組成 ── 為身、語、意之種子。

嘛呢 (Maṇi) 寶珠。

叭咪 (Padma) 蓮花。Padme 中之長音「e」為呼格,即梵文八格中之第八格 ── 呼喚之格,如孩童拖長聲呼「媽──」一般。

吽 (Hūṃ) 為勸請與召請之語。據諸經論所示,亦為心之種子字。

合而觀之,此咒乃為迎請: 「持寶珠與蓮花之尊！」 ── 此尊以水晶念珠與金色蓮花為手中標誌 ── 並向其覺心懇請: 「祈賜予我身、語、意之悉地！」

第二章 唵字與阿、烏、嘛三字

「唵」字之核心,為「阿」、「烏」、「嘛」三字。此三字承擔雙重意義 ── 既表不淨,亦表清淨;既為當下之我,亦為將成之佛。

兩種三門

◆ 行者之不淨三門

凡夫迷亂之身、語、意。

◆ 佛陀之清淨三門

覺者之身、語、意，亦稱「三金剛」。

關鍵問題在於：不淨三門能否轉變為清淨三門之本體？或二者本質截然不同？

佛法並不主張從本以來即有一實體，本來具足一切功德、永離一切過失。一切佛陀，昔皆與我等相同 ── 憑藉道路而成正覺。清淨之身、語、意，乃由漸次捨離不淨之性、轉成清淨之性而得。

成就此一轉化之道，即為咒中其餘四字所示 ── 「嘛呢」與「叭咪」（方便與智慧），以及「吽」（二者之合）。

第三章 嘛呢：菩提心之寶珠

如摩尼寶能滿眾生願，菩提心寶能成一切求解脫者之願。

「嘛呢」(Maṇi)意為「寶珠」，象徵道路中之「方便」分：菩提心、慈與悲。

正如珍寶能除貧苦，菩提心之寶能除一切衰退 ── 既除輪迴之衰，亦除小乘涅槃之滯。又如如意寶能滿眾生之願，菩提心能滿一切有情之究竟所求。故「嘛呢」字置於道之初門：若無慈悲為基，任何一步皆無方向。

第四章 叭咪：智慧之蓮花

「叭咪」(Padma)意為「蓮花」，象徵「智慧」。蓮花生於泥而不染於泥。同理，真實智慧能安住於無諸矛盾之境，不墮於二邊。若無智慧，矛盾必生。

諸智之中 ── 主要者

傳承中所說之智種類眾多：

  • 證 無常 之智；
  • 證 補特伽羅無我 之智；
  • 證 能所二取無別 之二空之智；
  • 證 自相空 之智。

於此一切智中，「叭咪」所指主要為 現證空性 之智 ── 即直觀面見 śūnyatā（空性）本身。

第五章 吽：不二之合

末字「吽」(Hūṃ)指示「不二之體」 ── 方便與智慧之合一。其義在示：心相續中諸過之淨化，必須依此合一而成 ── 唯方便不足，唯智慧亦不足。

二乘觀此合一

就顯教而言

此合一為「方便所攝之智慧」 ── 慈悲中含智見，智見中含慈悲。

就密咒乘（金剛乘）而言

指方便與智慧無分之究竟圓滿心識 ── 非二者並存，而為一體不二之覺。

又依五佛種子字，「吽」為 不動如來（Akṣobhya）之種子字 ── 不為任何法所動搖之尊。是故六字真言「唵嘛呢叭咪吽」教示：依方便與智慧合一之道而修，自身不淨之身、語、意，可徹底轉成佛之清淨身、語、意。

第二部

圓滿淨化體系

下表將四重淨化之義匯於一覽。橫向閱之，可見每一字如何同時對應一趣眾生、一種內在煩惱、一道之波羅蜜，並所顯之覺者部族。

第三部

修法與功德

第七章 觀修與念誦方法

傳承中之念誦法，根植於觀想。念誦並非僅為口誦，而是身、語、意一體相合之行。

觀想方法

觀上師與聖 Avalokiteśvara（觀世音菩薩）無二無別。其心間月輪上，觀白色種子字 Hrīḥ（舍） ── 此白色 Hrīḥ 字表觀世音菩薩之心；此字本即心之種子，源自梵文 hṛdaya，意為「心」、「精要」、「核心」。圍繞中央 Hrīḥ，觀六字環繞而轉。專注一意，如此而誦。

第八章 各字之功德

若如法持誦此六字明咒，功德不可思議。略言之，各字賜予一特定功德，如下表所列。最右一欄並示菩薩五道之相應位次 ── 由初地至究竟一切智位。

各字所授功德

由此一咒，「六根本煩惱得淨」、「六波羅蜜得圓」、「六佛部之覺德得顯」 ── 全道盡攝於一息之誦。

◆ ◆ ◆

結語
內具之種子

佛果不必外求。其因 ── 證得之潛能 ── 已具於自心相續之中。

正如怙主 Maitreya（彌勒）於《Mahāyānottaratantra》（《大乘無上續論》）中所言：一切眾生本具 如來藏（tathāgatagarbha）。圓滿果位由之而成之清淨種子 ── 即如來之精要 ── 已存於每一眾生之中。

六字 「唵嘛呢叭咪吽」 即為顯露此種子之直接方法。專注而誦，所求非遠 ── 乃對本已現前者之認識而已。

迴 向

願以此編集與閱誦之諸功德，

願一切眾生離苦及苦因，

速證聖觀自在之果位。
